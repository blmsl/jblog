Art insights

A glimpse behind my creative process

Visual art is core to who I am, and you’ll almost always find a me accompanied by a sketchpad of sorts which I’ll use to scribble on during downtime. I’m also the type of person who rarely leaves the house without a camera, because sometimes the best photographs are the more spontaneous ones.

Manifesto

There are four main goals I try to adhere to when I have my artist’s hat on:

  • Venture into the unknown. Never be afraid to experiment. Push the bounds of every toolset.
  • Challenge myself. Always improve upon previous works.
  • Contribute back. Create art not only for myself, but for the community as well.
  • Learn from others. Critique and provide constructive criticism that promotes growth.

On vector graphics

I create the bulk of my vector graphics using Inkscape. While I’ve used other vector editors in the past, I find myself returning to Inkscape as it works on all of my devices with minimum effort—for example, if I switch from my MacBook to one of the Linux or Windows devices I own, I don’t need to context switch or learn a completely new program.

I regularly find I need to set up a stable “working environment” before I can produce a work of any merit, and this usually comprises of a comfortable location with a drink—I have been known to disappear off to coffee shops when working on more complicated pieces.

My vector graphics can go from ultra-realistic to completely whimsical and absurd. For my personal images, the end result is usually dependent upon mood; for professional works I will stick to the description while adding my own personal touches where possible. If I’m ever unhappy with the way something is turning out, you’ll often see me drag what I’ve done off the side of the page and start again, using the previous attempt as a reference.

On photography

A lot of my photography is the correct mix of luck, lighting and determination. While I have a decent camera with a pretty bulky set of interchangeable lenses, I’ve always maintained that a good photographer can make any camera take an interesting photo.

My primary camera is a Canon EOS 650D, however I also own a SONY RX-100 point-and-shoot camera, and a Microsoft Lumia 950 phone with a 22 megapixel Carl Zeiss optics lens. Each one has its own set of characteristics, and I’ve found that I’ve been able to consistently take photos of similar quality on each of them.

When I started off working with photos, I worked with film. Obviously, this meant that I could only take a set number of photos before I had to pay for a new reel of film, but this has served as a great lesson on being efficient with photos I take. I learnt the value of knowing how a camera works before pressing the shutter release button, on how to get into the right positions to get the best lighting and how to essentially “manufacture” luck, or at least good conditions and circumstances to take a photo. I’ve never had the pleasure of being able to work in studio conditions; however I feel that this has helped rather than hindered me.

But what makes an artwork good?

The perception of whether a work is “good” or not depends on both the context and the viewer. For instance, a fine art painting may appear perfect framed upon a wall, but would probably look completely out of place when added into a marketing website about food. I will always attempt to create pieces that are both appealing and relevant in their intended context.

Catching the eye of a viewer is a much more complicated task. Knowing your audience is the keystone of a successful artwork. I’m always ready to engage with a community, find out their preferences and test the waters if required.
